The ElizaLang Language

I'm making up my own language!

This article doesn't deal with vocabulary. All words given are either English words or else nonsensical terms like 1es, both of which are used as placeholders for real ElizaLang words. This article also doesn't deal with the ElizaLang alphabet, punctuation marks or the shapes of the diacritical marks. Presently the letters are identified by their transcriptions into English only. The ElizaLang alphabet is a full alphabet though, which means the vowels are given their own letters rather than being merely diacritic marks like the short vowels in Arabic. There is no notion of capital and lower case letters.

Lexicon

The language uses a consonantal root system. Roots come from three sources.

  1. Biblical Hebrew, adjusted for the enlarged set of consonants using Semitic language cognates.
  2. Classical Arabic
  3. Latin words used in English

Where there are conflicting meanings between Hebrew and Arabic then the Hebrew meaning takes precedence. Words from Semitic origins never use /p/ in ElizaLang, only /f/. Latin root words are reduced to consonantal roots using a set process. These borrowed words may sometimes only exist in English when joined to an affix (e.g. re-volve), or may have experienced mutations due to adoption into English via French. In such cases the original Latin will first be restored. The twofold Semitic and Latinate sets of vocabulary form two distinct registers in the language. Almost every word is duplicated and language learners begin using the Latin vocabulary extensively and then gradually acquire more and more Semitic words. Proficient users may selectively use Latin derived words, phrases or sentences to give an item or an action a sense of jocularity, sarcasm, casualness, laxness, inferior status, lack of gravitas, lack of serious intention to complete an action, etc.

The word is syllabified in accordance with the permitted onset and coda consonant clusters in Classical Latin, with consonant clusters always split between syllables (even more so than in Latin, as in whenever a pair of valid coda and onsets can be formed), with any split needed occurring between the first and second consonants (or after the second consonant in the case of /skr/, which becomes /ʃ.r/).

A sequence of sound changes occurs. These are assumed to have taken place in ancient times inside the Latinate culture of the mythical constructed world (conworld) which our mythical group of Semitic people encountered.

  1. Loss of the aspirated consonants.
    • ph > f
    • th > θ
    • kh > k
  2. [+labialized] > [-labialized]w
  3. w > v except in syllable onsets kw, skw and gw
  4. ɫ > l
  5. ŋn > nː (or n when word initial after insertion of a new pattern)
  6. ŋ > n / _$
  7. sk > ʃ

z between vowels is always geminated, written with a diacritical mark in the native alphabet and transcribed as zz. A vowel followed by a nasal in the Latin orthography is realized as a vowel followed by a nasal and not as nasalized vowel.

A consonantal root is formed by extracting the onset and coda from each syllable of the intermediate word form. If the original word began with a vowel then the consonantal root will begin with a glottal stop. Thus, in borrowings combinations of consonants such as bl and br are treated as though they were a single consonant so far as insertion of patterns into roots is concerned. A prefix is added to words derived from Latin roots to indicate that they are borrowings (unless the root contains at least one /p/, /gw/, /kw/ or /ʃw/, since these are not used in words derived from Semitic origins).

Sentence Structure

Word Order

ElizaLang is a consistently head-first language.
  • Verb–Subject–Object (VSO)
  • Possessed–Possessor (NG)
  • Noun–Adjective (NA)
  • Noun-Quantifier

Markers

Adverbs and adjectives are consistently marked with an identical suffix to indicate their status as modifiers to a verb and to a noun respectively.

Gender

In ElizaLang nouns are not declined by gender and verbs are not conjugated by gender. Objects don't have genders and people shouldn't be judged by their genders!

Number & Definiteness

Nouns come before quantifiers. If no quantifier is given then the quantifiers "one" or "some" are assumed. Nouns are declined by definiteness and by number (singular or plural) and noun declensions must agree with any quantifier given. For example, "catuna two" would mean "two cats" if the words "cat" and "two" meant what they do in English but, "cat two", is invalid, just as "two cat" is in English.

The unknown number can also be used in the first person when the number is in fact known but the author wishes to leave it unspecified because the number of people may change over time, such as if additional people join a team to start writing in ElizaLang, c.f. the Royal "We".

Unlike English, pronouns in ElizaLang will remain the same in both the subject and the object positions, rather than creating constructions like, "He played him". In ElizaLang this would take the form, "Played 3as 3as." There will however be a morpheme similar to the English -self or -selves in order to create reflexive pronouns.

Quantities, Sets Operators & Predicates

If xP stands for any noun phrase without a quantifier then lists of things are described by the following grammar. Double negations are forbidden. 

FORMULA -> TERM | CONJUNCTION | DISJUNCTION

TERM -> SIMPLE_TERM | QUANTIFIED_PHRASE
SIMPLE_TERM -> xP | NEGATION
NEGATION -> not xP

QUANTIFIED_PHASE -> QUANTIFIED QUANTIFIER
QUANTIFIED -> SIMPLE_TERM | SIMPLE_CONJUNCTION | SIMPLE_DISJUNCTION
QUANTIFIER -> NUMBER | all | not-all | most | many | some | few | at-least1 | none

SIMPLE_CONJUNCTION -> and SIMPLE_TERM SIMPLE_TERM SIMPLE_TERM*
SIMPLE_DISJUNCTION -> or SIMPLE_TERM SIMPLE_TERM SIMPLE_TERM*

CONJUNCTION -> and TERM TERM COMPOUND_CONJUNCT*
CONJUNCTION -> and TERM COMPOUND_CONJUNCT COMPOUND_CONJUNCT*
CONJUNCTION -> and COMPOUND_CONJUNCT COMPOUND_CONJUNCT COMPOUND_CONJUNCT*

COMPOUND_CONJUNCT -> CONJUNCT_WORD TERM TERM TERM*
CONJUNCT_WORD -> and | or

DISJUNCTION -> or TERM TERM COMPOUND_DISJUNCT*
CONJUNCTION -> or TERM COMPOUND_DISJUNCT COMPOUND_DISJUNCT*
CONJUNCTION -> or COMPOUND_DISJUNCT COMPOUND_DISJUNCT COMPOUND_DISJUNCT*

COMPOUND_DISJUNCT -> and TERM TERM TERM*

Some examples of valid sentences:

  • come not alice → Alice doesn't come.
  • come and alice bob → Alice and Bob come.
  • come or alice bob → Alice or Bob come.
  • come and eve or alice bob → Eve and (Alice or Bob) come.
  • come and or eve billy or alice bob → (Eve or Billy) and (Alice or Bob) come.

If xP stands for any sentence then the following grammar produces conditional sentences. 

SENTENCE_FORMULA -> FORMULA | IMPLICATION | IMPLICATION_CONJUNCTION | IMPLICATION_DISJUNCTION

IMPLICATION -> if FORMULA then FORMULA
IMPLICATION_CONJUNCTION -> and IMPLICATION IMPLICATION+
IMPLICATION_DISJUNCTION -> or IMPLICATION IMPLICATION+

Counting

The counting system is base twelve and positional. There are distinct words and numerical digits for the numbers zero to eleven. As usual the actual words haven't been decided yet. There are also special words for numbers of the form 12n for n ∈ 3..11. These are formed by inflecting the word for n in some way. For now let's use the suffix ni, though the actual suffix hasn't been decided yet. So, threeni represents 123, fourni represents 124, and so on.

  1. To represent an integer between zero and eleven use the dedicated word for that number.
  2. To represent an integer between 1210 and 172710 (=123 - 1), or equivalently between 1012 and BBB12: take each numerical digit separately in order of most significant to least significant and output the dedicated word for the number corresponding to each digit.
  3. To represent an integer x larger than BBB12:

    1. Truncate x to three significant figures (in base twelve) to obtain x′.

      Technically this step is optional, but in ElizaLang it's highly, highly unusual to read aloud or write out long strings of numbers like we have in English, for example, seven hundred and sixty-eight thousand five hundred and thirty-nine. It's unusual to the point of being insulting. Although in English we seldom write out long sequences of words like this we do use them in speech.

    2. Identify the maximum possible integer n ∈ 3..11 and integers m ∈ 1..11 and r ∈ 0..12n-1 such that x′ = m × 12n + r.

      If m = 1 then you have the option of going back and deriving a new x′ with four significant figures if you wish.

    3. If m ≠ 1 then output the word for m.
    4. Output the inflected word for n.
    5. If r ≠ 0:
      1. Taking each numerical digit of r separately,
      2. drop any zero digits in the least significant positions so that the resulting list of digits doesn't end in a 0, and,
      3. output a word for each remaining digit in order of most significant to least significant by outputting the dedicated word for the number corresponding to that digit.

A possible extension to the system (though perhaps unnecessary) is to use zeroni to represent 1212, oneni to represent 1213 and twoni to represent 1214 (in base 12, 1010, 1011 and 1012).

Essentially the system for expressing large numbers works somewhat like scientific notation in mathematics, where a number like 1728 would be written as 1.728 × 103. In ElizaLang the -ni word acts as both the equivalent of the decimal point and the equivalent of the ×103 part, although everything is in base twelve in ElizaLang. The convention of dropping of the word for "one" where it would otherwise occur before a -ni word is inspired by computerized representations of numbers (IEEE 754).
