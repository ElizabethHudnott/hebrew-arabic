Elizabeth's Language

This article doesn't deal with the language's alphabet and punctuation marks. Here the letters are identified by their romanizations only.

In a few cases the headings on the chart are approximate, perhaps reflecting sound changes that occurred during transition period prior to those addressed by this document. The glottal stop is omitted in romanizations when it occurs at the beginning of a word. Every consonant can be geminated, which occurs when the same consonant functions as both the coda to one syllable and as the onset of the next. This is generally shown in romanization by doubling the sound's romanized letter although digraphs are not doubled. In romanizations a full stop appears in the middle of a word immediately before a digraph when the previous syllable is open and the romanizations of the individual letters of the diagraph correspond to romanizations of other sounds.

[oː] is the replacement for an earlier [aw] sound. /j/ is romanized as i when it appears in a coda, thus forming ai, aai, ei and ui. That last one is pronounced as [ui].

Historical Sound Changes

  1. aw, aːw > o
    • i > e
    • iː > eː
    • w > v

Syllable Structures

The permissible syllable structures are Cv and CvC. A choice of vowels when combined with possibilities for consonant gemination forms a template. Templates can only include /eː/ and /uː/ in open syllables, although when a template containing /u/ is inserted into a root with where the result includes u from the template followed by v from root, where that consonant was formerly pronounced as [w], then the result can be /u/ lengthening to /uː/ and a re-romanization from uv into uu. Inserting a triconsonantal root into a template produces one of following forms.

Sentence Structure

The language is consistently head-first.
  • Verb–Subject–Object (VSO)
  • Possessed–Possessor (NG)
  • Noun–Adjective (NA)
  • Noun-Quantifier

Derivational Morphology

Adverbs and adjectives are consistently marked with a suffix to indicate their statuses as modifiers to verbs and nouns respectively.

Gender

Nouns are not marked for masculine or feminine gender and verbs are not conjugated according to human gender. Objects don't have gender roles and people shouldn't be judged by their sex or gender! However, nouns are assigned to one of two grammatical genders.

  • Logical, understood, mechanical
  • Irrational, emotional, mysterious

Copula

There is a copula verb and its use is always mandatory. It conjugates the same way as other verbs.

Lexicon Overview

The language uses a consonantal root system. Roots come from two sources.

  1. Biblical Hebrew, adjusted for the enlarged set of consonants using Semitic language cognates.
  2. Classical Arabic.

Where the Hebrew and the Arabic have conflicting meanings then the Hebrew meaning takes precedence.
