The ElizaLang Language

I'm making up my own language!

This article doesn't deal with the alphabet, punctuation marks or shapes of the diacritical marks. Presently the letters are identified by their transcriptions into English only. The forthcoming alphabet is a full alphabet though, which means vowels are given their own letters rather than being merely diacritic marks like the short vowels in Arabic. There is no notion of capital and lower case letters.

Lexicon

The language uses a consonantal root system. Roots come from two sources.

  1. Biblical Hebrew, adjusted for the enlarged set of consonants using Semitic language cognates.
  2. Classical Arabic.

Where there are conflicting meanings between Hebrew and Arabic then the Hebrew meaning takes precedence.

Syllable Structures

The permissible syllable structures are CV, CVV, CVC and CVVC.

Sentence Structure

 The language is consistently head-first.
  • Verb–Subject–Object (VSO)
  • Possessed–Possessor (NG)
  • Noun–Adjective (NA)
  • Noun-Quantifier

Morphology

Adverbs and adjectives are consistently marked with an identical suffix to indicate their statuses as modifiers to verbs and as modifiers to nouns respectively.

Gender

Nouns are not declined by gender and verbs are not conjugated by gender. Objects don't have genders and people shouldn't be judged by their genders!

Number & Definiteness

Nouns come before quantifiers. If no quantifier is given then the quantifiers "one" or "some" are assumed. Nouns are declined by definiteness and by number (singular or plural) and noun declensions must agree with any quantifier given. For example, "catuna two" would mean "two cats" if the words "cat" and "two" meant what they do in English but, "cat two", is invalid, just as "two cat" is in English.

Counting

The counting system is base six and positional. As usual the actual words haven't been decided yet. There are also special words for numbers of the form 6n for n ∈ 3..5. These are formed by inflecting the word for n in some way. For now let's use the suffix ni, though the actual suffix hasn't been decided yet. So, threeni represents 63, fourni represents 64 and fiveni represents 65.

  1. To represent an integer between zero and five use the dedicated word for that number.
  2. To represent an integer between 610 and 21510 (=63 - 1), or equivalently between 106 and 5556: take each numerical digit separately in order of most significant to least significant and output the dedicated word for the number corresponding to each digit.
  3. To represent an integer x larger than 5556:

    1. Truncate x to three significant figures (in base six) to obtain x′.

      Technically this step is optional, but in ElizaLang it's highly, highly unusual to read aloud or write out long strings of numbers like we have in the English example seven hundred and sixty-eight thousand five hundred and thirty-nine. It's unusual to the point of being insultingly pedantic. Although in English we seldom write out such long sequences of words we do use them in speech.

    2. Identify the highest possible integer n ∈ 3..5 and integers m ∈ 1..5 and r ∈ 0..6n-1 such that x′ = m × 6n + r.

      If m = 1 then you have the option of going back and deriving a new x′ with four significant figures if you wish.

    3. If m ≠ 1 then output the word for m.
    4. Output the inflected word for n.
    5. If r ≠ 0:
      1. Taking each numerical digit of r separately,
      2. drop any zero digits in the least significant positions so that the resulting list of digits doesn't end in a 0, and,
      3. output a word for each remaining digit in order from the most significant to the least significant by outputting the dedicated word for the number corresponding to that digit.

A possible extension to the system is to use zeroni to represent 66, oneni to represent 67 and twoni to represent 68 (in base 6 expressed as 1010, 1011 and 1012).

Essentially the system for expressing numbers larger than 215 works like scientific notation in mathematics, where a number like 1728 would be written as 1.728 × 103. In ElizaLang the -ni word acts both as the equivalent of the decimal point and as the equivalent of the ×103 part, although everything is in base six in ElizaLang. The convention of dropping of the word for "one" where it would otherwise occur before a -ni word is inspired by computerized representations of numbers (IEEE 754).
