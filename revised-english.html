  • When two single letter sounds occur next to each other in a way that could be mistaken for a two letter sound then a ' character is inserted to clarify that two one-letter sounds are intended. For example, t'h distinguishes t followed by h from the th sound.
  • I suggest we attempt to undo historical accent changes which have resulted in the /g/ sound becoming dropped from some words like singer in many accents. The older pronunciation is still preserved in Birmingham, Manchester, Liverpool and New York. Accordingly, as proposed, ng should always be spoken as /ŋg/ in our newly formed prestige accent, and taught as such to those who are learning English for the first time. Nonetheless, if some existing speakers continue pronounce some words without the /g/ sound then there is no loss because the ng spelling (with a silent g in this case) remains the same as the current spelling convention. Either way, any g or k placed immediately after an n indicates that the alternative pronunciation for the n should be used and therefore a separate letter for /ŋ/ is not needed.

Yod Dropping

The letter y is not included alongside oo in the following cases, where only a small minority of accents such as Welsh English (and a few US accents) preserve a distinct sound, which I've chosen not to reflect in the revised spelling. Other accents simply have an /uː/ ("oo") sound in this position. Also, due to the omission of any symbol to represent this additional /ɪʊ̯/ vowel, words such as yew and you become homographs in the revised spelling, despite their unique pronunciations in Welsh English. Both are spelled yoo in the revised spelling. Yod dropping only occurs when the /j/ sound and the consonant before it both occur within the same syllable. For a contrary example, consider the word volume, which has a revised spelling of volyoom despite many other words lacking a y after an l.
