When two single letter sounds occur next to each other in a way that could be mistaken for a two letter sound then a · character is inserted to clarify that two one-letter sounds are intended and not the one two-letter sound. For example, the word knighthood would be spelled nyt·húd to make clear that it does not involve a th, /θ/ sound.
  3. I suggest that we attempt to undo the historical changes in accent that have resulted in the /g/ sound becoming dropped from some words like singer in many accents (although the older pronunciation is preserved in Birmingham, Manchester, Liverpool and New York). The revised spelling proposal is that ng should always be spoken as /ŋg/, at least for those who are now learning English for the first time.

Less Commonly Distinguished Sounds

Some accents have retained certain old fashioned features that were once the norm but now no longer exist in Standard Southern British pronunciation. There are two approaches that our spelling design could take here.

  1. To decide that these features are worth preserving and to make provision to include them in the new spelling.
  2. To decide that the modern Standard Southern British accent itself represents a standard for English learners to aim for (for example, because it's internationally recognizable as the modern version of Received Pronunciation), and that we can keep things simple by omitting certain distinctions where two former sounds have merged into a single sound in Southern England. After all, this section will confuse many English speakers from England, let alone people trying to learn English as a second language.

At the present time I am undecided between these two alternatives. I'm a big fan of history and on the one hand, and I'm keen to preserve it and think that maybe us Southern Englanders should make the effort to learn about a little about British pronunciation history and maybe even "correct" certain aspects of our Southern English accents to sound a little more like our Scottish and Northern Irish counterparts who've managed to preserve this history. On the other hand, the whole point of the revised spelling project is to produce a simplified version of the English language so why not go all out and simplify things as much as possible? After all, Southern British people don't seem to ever mistake a fir tree for a fur tree in conversation, because the context is clear.

As a compromise, to reflect these pronunciation differences I've actually developed two spelling conventions for certain tricky English words: the Precise Revised Spelling and the Simple Revised Spelling.
