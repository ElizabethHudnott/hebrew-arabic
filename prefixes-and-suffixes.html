The Definite Article: "The"

Hebrew

To take any word, say like, מֶלֶךְ (melekh, "king") and turn it into the definite form ("the king") we attach a prefix onto the beginning of the word. This differs from English where "the" is written as a separate word. The rule basically has two parts, with some exceptional cases recorded in .

  1. Add the letter ה to beginning of the word, usually with a patach, הַ.
  2. Add a doubling dagesh to what was originally the first letter of the word, unless that letter is a gutteral.

Hence, for מֶלֶךְ, melekh, we derive the word הַמֶּלֶךְ, hammelekh.

If the first letter originally had a hardening dagesh then the same dot usually changes its meaning to take the role of a doubling dagesh instead of a hardening one and the pronunciation of the consonant changes. Thus, ‎בָּקָר (baqar, "cattle") becomes הַבָּקָר, which is pronounced as havvaqar in the dialects that have distinct pronunciations for בּ and ב and habbaqar otherwise. A dagesh after a vowel is always a doubling dagesh unless the letter forms the beginning of a stressed syllable (in which case it will be both, e.g. shabbat).

Arabic

Definite articles are simpler in Arabic. The definite article is written using the prefix ٱل. Formal texts such as the Qur'an include the small letter ص written over the alif, known as Hamza Wasl (not to be confused with the regular Hamza Qatʻ). If the previous word ends in a vowel then the alif is not pronounced. The first letter of the original word is doubled if it's a sun letter, which is indicated using a shaddah. The lam becomes silent also in this case. Otherwise, when the lam is pronounced then it has a sukoon. Some authors transcribe the definite article into English as al- regardless of whether the the alif or the lam would be pronounced in Arabic speech.
